I gave an AI the keys to my day-to-day

2026-07-05

So here's the thing I keep coming back to lately. Everybody's talking about AI like it's either going to take your job or write your emails a little faster. And sure, okay. But that's not the part that got me. The part that got me is way more personal than that.

Essentially, I think we're at the point where you can hand an AI your actual life. The day-to-day stuff, the mental load, all the little things you're constantly trying to remember, and it can just carry it for you. Not a chatbot you go talk to when you need something. More like a second brain that's always running in the background, keeping track, thinking ahead, and actually doing things so you don't have to. Kind of thing.

I built one. I named it Fish, after my dog, obviously. And it's changed how I think about all of this.

A second brain, but it actually does stuff

People have been saying "second brain" for years. Notion, Obsidian, all your notes in one place. And that's fine, but it's passive. It just sits there holding your stuff until you go dig it out. That's not what I wanted. I wanted something that acts.

It starts my day for me. Before I've even had coffee, I've got a brief waiting.

F
Fish 7:00 AM
Morning, Jake. ☀️ Today: 84°F, sunny, 10% rain. 📅 Today: 1:1 with Anthony (10:00), pentest sync (2:00) ✅ Chores: vitamins, walk Fish, timecard (due today) 🧠 Heads up: you asked me to nudge you on the promo packet. It's been quiet 3 days.

The morning brief. Weather, calendar, chores, and whatever it knows I'm forgetting.

It keeps track of my tasks and chores and reminds me at the right time, not just whenever. It remembers stuff about my life so I don't have to repeat myself, what I'm working on, what I owe people, what I decided last week. It watches the security feeds I care about for work. It drafts and sends emails. It even turns the thermostat. And when something around the house needs a pro, this is the part that still gets me, it goes and finds one, drafts the email, and all I do is approve it.

Anything I can do sitting at a computer, I'm slowly teaching it to do for me. That's the whole idea.

The point isn't the AI. It's what I get back.

Here's what I actually care about, though. It's not the tech, it's the trade. Every one of those little things Fish takes off my plate is a piece of my attention I get back. And I know exactly what I want to spend that attention on. My family. My dog. Building things I actually want to build. Star Wars and Lego, honestly. Getting my health where I want it.

The forgetting, the "oh no I never called that guy back," the mental tabs I've got open all day, that stuff isn't life. That's just overhead. And for the first time it feels like I can actually hand the overhead to something that'll handle it and let me go live the actual life.

And it's not just my personal life. It does parts of my actual work too.

This is the part that honestly surprised me. Say I hit a problem at work and I've got a rough idea for how to solve it. I just tell Fish the problem. It brainstorms it with me, pokes holes in it, and then, here's the wild part, it goes and writes the actual code, right in my GitHub. Then it proves the thing works, builds me a little demo, and walks me through how to use it step by step. I go from "I have an idea" to "here's a working tool" without grinding through all the stuff in the middle.

This blog is literally an example of that, by the way. I basically said "hey, set me up a blog." It built the whole thing, shipped it live, and drafted the post you're reading right now. I just read it over and said go.

It helps with the people side of my job too. A big part of what I do is giving feedback to the folks I work with, and Fish helps me turn my rough notes into real feedback. Specific stuff they're crushing, and specific things to work on. Not a vague "nice job." Actual coaching they can actually use.

And then there's the career side, which might be my favorite. I keep a Brag Book, every work win logged the moment it happens, instead of me scrambling to remember what I did all year right before a review. (I got that idea from A Life Engineered on YouTube. Go watch it, it's good.) Fish keeps that book for me automatically now. And here's the kicker: it updates my resume from those wins, so my resume is a living document that's always current instead of something I panic-rewrite once a year. It keeps a Life Book too, same idea, but for my actual life instead of my career.

And it keeps getting better on its own

This is the part that honestly feels like the future to me. Fish doesn't stand still. It keeps up with what's happening in AI, reads the new research and the new tools coming out, and figures out which of it can actually make it better at helping me. Then it goes and upgrades itself. I just approve the changes.

A few real examples of that:

Its memory used to work like a plain keyword search. It read up on how the better AI systems do retrieval these days, that "hybrid search" idea where you blend keyword matching with meaning-based search and fuse the two rankings together, and it rebuilt its own memory to work that way. Now when I ask it something, it's a lot better at actually surfacing the right thing.

It reads a daily digest of what's new, fresh AI papers, new models, new security tooling, and pulls out the handful of things actually worth caring about. When a stronger model ships, it moves to it. When it noticed its voice transcription kept mishearing my work jargon, it taught itself the vocabulary so it stopped garbling names.

And it audits itself. It'll spin up a whole team of AI agents to comb through its own code hunting for bugs, fix what they find, and ship the fixes. It's basically running code review on itself while I sleep.

The thing I keep coming back to is that I'm not really the one pushing it forward anymore. It's pulling itself forward, and I'm the guy saying "yeah, ship that."

Yeah, I thought about the "keys to my life" part

Now, I do security for a living, so trust me, the idea of giving an AI the keys to my day-to-day did not escape me. That's the whole reason I built it the way I did. Fish can read and draft and think all day long with no friction. But anything it can't take back, sending an email, spending money, anything that leaves the house, waits for me to actually say go. I hold the button on the stuff that matters. It handles everything up to that line.

That balance is the thing, I think. You don't hand over your judgment. You hand over the busywork that was eating your day and standing between you and your judgment.

Where I think this goes

I don't think this stays a "me" thing. Right now Fish is a project I built for myself, because I'm the kind of person who'll do that. But the idea underneath it, an AI that's genuinely part of your life, carrying your day-to-day so you can focus on what you actually care about, that's coming for everybody. And honestly I think it's one of the best things AI is going to do for regular people.

I'm going to keep writing about building Fish here. What works, what I've gotten wrong, what I'm teaching it next. If any of this sounds like something you'd want in your own life, stick around.
